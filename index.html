About Me

I’m Ding Wang, a Project Scientist in the UCLA School of Medicine. My work is driven by a deep curiosity about how biosystems work at the molecular level and how this knowledge can help us better understand human health and disease. I began my scientific training at Peking University, where I earned my BS and MS degrees in organic and analytical chemistry. I then completed my PhD at the University of Washington with Dr. Michael Gelb, developing quantitative proteomics methods to analyze clinical samples used in newborn screening for congenital disorders.

My growing interest in omics-based biomedical research led me to UCLA for postdoctoral training with Dr. Peipei Ping. There, I helped build advanced proteomics approaches to study cardiovascular disease in both patients and experimental models. I later joined the faculty as a Project Scientist and have now spent more than a decade as a senior member of the Ping research group. Over the years, I have developed expertise in proteomics, mitochondrial biology, redox signaling, and cardiovascular disease, along with extensive experience in lab management, grant support, and collaborative research.

At UCLA, one of my major contributions has been helping develop a comprehensive proteomics pipeline that maps how heart proteins change over time—capturing the molecular dynamics that shape both healthy and diseased states. Working closely with data science colleagues and trainees, I’ve also helped create computational tools that reveal protein structures, turnover rates, and responses to stress. Most recently, our team has incorporated deep-learning and AI approaches to overcome key challenges in large-scale omics datasets and to predict how genetic variations, such as SNPs, influence global patterns of protein post-translational modifications.

These combined efforts have resulted in more than 30 publications in journals including Nature Scientific Data, JCI, Circulation, Circulation Research, and eLife, along with a book chapter on mitochondrial apoptosis and autophagy.
